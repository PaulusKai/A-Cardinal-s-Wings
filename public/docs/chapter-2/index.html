    2.0 Agency Problem? Be Proactive#

    TLDR#

    “My thoughts be bloody, or be nothing worth!”
    — Hamlet

    “We can always choose our response.”
    — Victor Frankl

    Being proactive means focusing on what we can control, accepting what we cannot, and choosing our actions based on our values instead of exploding like a diet-coke Mentos bottle rocket.

    Life is too short for pointless fights.

    Time spent kicking, yelling, and overthinking things outside our control is time not spent on things we can control.

    A proactive person does not ask:

    “Why didn’t life give me what I expected?”

    They ask:

    “It doesn’t matter what I expect of life. What does life expect of me?”

    1. What Does It Mean to Be Proactive?#

    There are parts of life we cannot control.

    We cannot change our spawn points like the Minecraft speedrunners. But…

    we can control our response!

    Being proactive means recognizing your agency in a situation and acting with intention.

    It means making choices based on your values instead of reacting to every external impulse, insult, fear, or doubt. We are humans not diet-coke Mentos rockets that go crazy.

    A reactive person says:

    “This happened to me, so now I’m helpless AHHHH…”

    A proactive person says:

    “Ok this just happened. Why did this go wrong? What can I do next?”

    You cannot always control how you feel, but you can always decide what to do next.

    Either be the jailer of your own cell, or walk through your open prison door.

    2. To Be Proactive Means to Focus on What You Can Control#

    It is easy to get stuck in thoughts like:

    “What if I did this differently? If life was more fair?”
    “BRO! When will everyone finally recognize that I’m the goat and start glazing me?”

    Be warned! I tend to find that this tends to snowball on itself with its addictive fantasies! Ruminating on this leads to more fantasy, self-pity, or pointless fighting.

    According to my experience, mulling over these thoughts is a waste of time.

    A better practice is:

    Worry about what you control.
    Accept the rest.

    Life can take opportunities from us.

    Fate can close doors that might have made us better.

    But what good is it to live inside imaginary alternate timelines or have conversations with people who don’t exist?

    The past is a place to learn from, not a place to live in.

    Give quality time for reflection, but not so much that it causes more problems.

    3. When We Are Proactive, We Choose Our Responses#

    If someone says:

    “I’m better than you at ___.”

    We have a choice. (It helps to think of these dialogues like a turn-based game.)

    Bogus response:

    1. You can slug them. Why? They asked for it! RAHH!

    2. Or you can carry that comment around all day:“AH someone hurt me! Waah (baby sounds)”

    3. Or you can simply think:

    “Okay.”

    Then move on. We don’t need to fight unnecessary wars in our heads.

    Yeah, a lot of energy is wasted because we take everything personally.

    But if you fight tooth and nail over every little thing, you will be too tired for the things that actually matter.

    4. Example: I Didn’t Get into Harvard#

    Say I worked extremely hard but did not get into Harvard.

    I have a choice:

    “This is so unfair. I should have gotten in. Everyone else is lucky. Everything I do is somehow wrong and everything they do is right.”

    (Proceeds to cry.)

    “This hurts, but life does not care what I expected. What does life expect of me now? I should learn from my mistakes, cheer on others around me, and keep moving forward.”

    Doesn’t Option 2 sound way better? In fact, that sounds lwk chad!

    We don’t pretend that disappointment doesn’t hurt.

    We just don’t let it spill and make ourselves a mess. Just because we messed up a math problem doesn’t mean we need to tear it up, soak it in our tears, and eat it for dinner! We just storage it or recycle it.

    5. Proactive Students Admit Their Ignorance and Ask Questions#

    This is especially powerful in AP classes, where many people suffer from Imposter Syndrome.

    Let’s call that the “AP Imposter Syndrome.”

    What does it sound like?

    “That person looks like she knows everything.”
    “Everyone will think I’m stupid if I ask.”
    “I shouldn’t ask my question. It’s dumb.”

    Don’t get me wrong. Yes, people in AP classes are smart.

    But aren’t they taking the class because they don’t know everything?

    Worrying about other people’s opinions is worrying about things we can’t control yet.

    If we are proactive, we can choose to just ask our questions.

    If you ask the question, here’s what happens:

    1. The teacher teaches better.

    2. Other students become less afraid to ask their questions too, which will benefit the whole class.

    Know that regardless of how much value you get out of class, you are spending the same life force, your time and maybe money also, to attend class!

    But if you ask questions, you might get 10x the value.

    And those smart-looking classmates are probably not thinking about you, but shaking in their chairs from the same problem you’re facing. (More on this in the next chapter on fear, but you probably get the point.)

    Isn’t that worth the shot?

    6. Don’t Overthink It#

    Overthinking happens when the more you think, the harder the problem looks.

    How do you know when you are overthinking?

    A useful boundary I use is:

    If I think something over more than three times before doing something, then I tell myself I’m probably overthinking.

    Try this out!

    At some point, doing the thing gives better information than thinking about it.

    Sometimes, we can’t see the benefits until we get our head out of the loop.

    7. Dealing with Black/White Thinking#

    Sometimes we get reactive because our emotions take over. This happens to me quite a lot!

    It looks like this:

    Before we do something we care about: “If this goes badly, everything is over.”

    When getting insulted: “Everyone is out to get me. Don’t trust those bums.”
    When messing up: “I SUCK! I’m the world’s greatest loser.”
    When we succeed: “I’m the world’s greatest winner. The goat!” Then after celebrating: “That person did way better than me. I’m horrible!”

    This makes us do worse actually, because we’re putting our attention on our negative thoughts and not what’s actually going on. We’re putting unnecessary stakes on ourselves, and it makes us our own nemesis when we get hurt.

    We end up multiplying our own damage on ourselves, turning one point of damage into DOT damage! Isn’t it kinda weird to CHOOSE to hurt ourselves more?

    And it’s hard to motivate ourselves when we are bogged down in these problems.

    “Ok bro, how do we deal with this?”

    We deal with this not by ignoring our emotions or suppressing them.

    Instead, we should:

    • Recognize this type of thinking. Let it come like an old friend.
    • Ask yourself “what is this emotion telling me?”
    • Also, Journal it if helpful or Talk to trusted friends and adults.

    8. Being Proactive Is Hard#

    Being proactive sounds simple, but it is easier said than done. What are some obstacles?

    • You are tired, like coming out of back-to-back tests.
    • You do not know what the next step is because we’re in new territory.
    • You have bottled-up negative feelings.
    • (Important!) You distract yourself with devices instead of processing your emotions. (More in chapter 4!)

    So yeah, do not expect yourself to always enter main character mode through pure willpower. It takes quite a lot of energy/mana!

    Here are our thoughts for each obstacle:

    • Push yourself and judge yourself fairly.
    • Ask others for help. See Chapter 3 for more.
    • Check out Section 7 above.
    • Fix your environment. See Chapters 4 and 5.

    9. Some Final Thoughts#

    Proactivity is not taking everything personally, not overthinking, and choosing our best actions.

    Life will throw you garbage that’s outside of your expectations. When you are there, ask yourself:

    “What does life expect of me?”
