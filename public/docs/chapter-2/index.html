    2. Stuck overthinking? → Be proactive!

    TLDR “My thoughts be bloody, or be nothing worth!” — Hamlet

    “We can always choose our response.” – Victor Frankl Being proactive means focusing on what we can control, accepting what we cannot, and choosing our actions based on our values instead of reacting emotionally to every situation. Life is too short for pointless fights. Time spent kicking, yelling, and overthinking things outside our control is time not spent on things we can control. A proactive person does not ask, “Why didn’t life give me what I expected?” They ask: It doesn’t matter what I expect of life. What does life expect of me?

    1. What Does It Mean to Be Proactive? There are parts of life we cannot control. We cannot change our “spawn point” like Minecraft speedrunners. But we can control our response. Being proactive means recognizing your agency in a situation and acting with intention. It means making choices based on your values instead of reacting to every external impulse, insult, fear, or doubt. A reactive person says: “This happened to me, so now I’m helpless.” A proactive person says: “This happened. What can I do next?” You cannot always control how you feel, but you can always decide what to do next. Either to be the jailer of your own cell, or to walk through your open prison door.
    2. To be proactive means to focus on what you can control It is easy to get stuck in thoughts like: “What if I did this differently?” “What if life was more fair?” “When will everyone finally recognize that I’m the goat and start glazing me?” But too much of this becomes fantasy, self-pity, or pointless fighting. According to my experience, mulling over these thoughts is a waste of time. A better practice is: Worry about what you control. Accept the rest. Life can take opportunities from us. Fate can close doors that might have made us better. But what good is it to live inside imaginary alternate timelines or have conversations with people who don’t exist? The past is a place to learn from, not a place to live in. Give quality time for reflection, but don’t so much as to cause more problems.
    3. When we are proactive, we can choose our responses

    If someone says: “I’m better than you at ___.” We have a choice. (It helps to think of these dialogues similar to a turn-based game.) (Bogus response) You can Slug them. They deserve it! Carry that comment around all day. AH someone hurt me! Or you can simply think: “Okay.” Then move on. This does not mean you become passive. It means you stop fighting unnecessary wars. A lot of energy is wasted because we take everything personally. But if you fight tooth and nail over every little thing, you will be too tired for the things that actually matter. 4. Example: I didn’t get into Harvard Say you worked extremely hard but did not get into Harvard. You have a choice: “This is so unfair. I should have gotten in. Everyone else is lucky. Everything I do is somehow wrong and everything they do is right.” (proceeds to cry)

    “This hurts, but life does not care what I expected. What does life expect of me now? I should learn from my mistakes, cheer on others around me, and keep moving forward. Doesn’t 2) sound way better? We don’t pretend that disappointment doesn’t hurt. We just don’t let it spill and make ourselves a mess.

    1. Proactive students admit their ignorance and ASK questions

    This is especially powerful in AP classes, where many people suffer from Imposter Syndrome. Let’s call that the “AP Imposter Syndrome.” What does it sound like?

    “That person looks like she knows everything.” “Everyone will think I’m stupid if I ask .” “I shouldn’t ask my question. It’s dumb.”

    Don’t get me wrong. Yes people in AP classes are smart, but aren’t they taking the class because they DON’T know everything?

    Worrying about how other’s opinions is worrying about things we can’t control (yet). If we are proactive, we can choose to just ask our questions! If you ask the question, you get more value from class. The teacher teaches better. Other students become less afraid to ask their questions too, which will benefit the whole class. Either way, you are spending the same lifeforce to attend class. But if you ask questions, you might get 10x the value. And those smart-looking classmates are probably not thinking about you, but shaking in their chairs from the same problem you’re facing. Isn’t that worth the shot? (more on this in the next chapter on fear) 6. Don’t overthink it

    Overthinking happens when the more you think, the harder the problem looks. How do you know when we are overthinking?

    A useful boundary I use is: If I think something over more than three times before doing something, then I tell myself I’m probably overthinking.

    Try this out! At some point, doing the thing gives better information than thinking about it. Sometimes, we can’t see the benefits until we get our head

    1. Dealing with black/white thinking

    Sometimes we get reactive because our emotions take over. It looks like:

    (When getting insulted) “Everyone is out to get me.” (When we mess up) “I’m the world’s greatest loser (When we succeed) “I’m the world’s greatest winner. The goat!” (Before we do something we care about) “If this goes badly, everything is over.”

    This puts unnecessary stakes on ourselves, and it makes us our own nemesis when we get hurt. We end up multiplying our own damage on ourselves. And it’s hard to motivate ourselves when we are bogged down in these problems.

    We deal with this not by ignoring our emotions. Instead, we should:

    Recognize this type of thinking Ask yourself why you feel that way (journal it if helpful) Talk to trusted friends and adults

    These should help you decide your next best move.

    1. Being Proactive Is Hard Being proactive sounds simple, but it is not always easy. It gets harder when: You are tired (like coming out of back-to-back tests.) You do not know what the next step is. You have bottled-up negative feelings. You distract yourself with devices instead of processing your emotions. So do not expect yourself to always enter “main character mode” through pure willpower. Here’s our thoughts for each obstacle: Push yourself and judge yourself fairly. Ask others for help. (See chapter 3 for more) Check out section 7 above! Fix your environment (see chapter 4,5)
    2. Some Final thoughts

    Proactivity is not taking everything personally, not overthinking, and choosing our best actions.

    Life will throw you garbage that’s out of your expectations. When you are there, ask yourself “what does life expect of me?”
