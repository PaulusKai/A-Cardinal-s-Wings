    4.1 Bingeing? Scrolling Too Much? Solutions!#

    “In the throes of desire, there’s no deciding. Binding ourselves is a way to be free from temptation.”

    — Anna Lembke

    TLDR#

    If you want to stop bingeing, do not just fight cravings in the moment.

    Instead, accept some boredom, know that it’s natural to feel horrible after a flood of dopamine, and build systems that protect you before desire takes over.

    Tbh, the engineers at Youtube, TikTok, Insta, etc… – THEY ARE TOO SMART. Now, I don’t even bother fighting against their beautifully addictive algorithm. I feel it’s smarter to avoid it entirely. We are smart enough to know that when we watch 1 hour of videos, the first 10 minutes will be good stuff and the 50 other minutes will be brainrot.

    The Problem#

    “Oh I… just scrolled five hours of YouTube! AHH… I feel like a loser! Like bro, it’s not even fun after the third hour, and yet I still do this!”

    We feel you, we’ve done the same.

    So how should we solve this problem?

    A bogus solution is “oh, I’m just lazy and I’m just a loser.”

    Nah. You are not powerless; you can change this by being proactive. Here are three keys that can help.

    1. Understand Why We Binge or Doom-Scroll#

    Tech is designed to be addictive.

    Literally, there are papers and dedicated scientists locking in to make already addictive platforms like YouTube even more addictive.

    They are trying their utmost to get you to watch “just one more video.”

    They are trying to recommend content that is designed to be consumed, not necessarily content that is designed to help you.

    Remember their goal: They want your attention.

    Your attention equals their salaries, which equal their calories next winter.

    Even after years of watching, pleasure never really feels like “enough.”

    Isn’t that some toxic design, bro?

    2. Boredom and Loneliness Make the Internet More Appealing#

    Modern technology is built to be addictive.

    But boredom and loneliness make it even more tempting.

    Elisa put it well:

    “I truly believe the internet is so addicting because we are bored and lonely in real life. You need to have a very fun life for the internet to not look fun.”

    Social proof also makes excessive doom-scrolling seem normal. After all, we humans are social creatures.

    More often than we would like to admit, we follow the crowd. That is why it is important to stick with people who lift you up.

    3. Feeling Bad After Bingeing is Normal#

    If you feel horrible after wasting a few hours, or even a whole day, that is normal,

    We often feel horrible after too much pleasure because of homeostasis. Maybe you learned homeostasis in freshman biology class.

    Specifically, the pleasure-pain balance can help us understand what is happening.

    That was one of the only things I remembered, and it is one of the most important parts of this solution.(Thanks, Ms. Killpack!)

    Homeostasis: Your Body Wants Balance#

    Homeostasis is your body trying to restore balance.

    For example, when you get hot, your body tries to cool down by sweating. When you get cold, your body shivers to warm back up.

    When you exercise like a demon, your body replies by sweating buckets. When you take a cold shower, your body may feel super warm afterward.

    This is the important part:

    Just like your body balances hot and cold, it also balances pleasure and pain.

    If you press hard on the pleasure side by bingeing five hours of YouTube or gaming all night, your body will reply by making you feel miserable. We’ll think “oh I’m a LOSER! I just wasted SO much time!”

    On the other hand, if you press on the pain side through something like working out, your body may reply by making you feel great afterward.

    If you are in physics, you can think of it like comparing kinetic energy and gravitational potential energy when throwing a ball upward. What comes up must come down.

    But, here’s where the design of our devices get dangerous…

    Repeated Bingeing Makes the Cycle Worse#

    (From Dopamine Nation)

    Our situation gets dangerous when we scroll and binge repeatedly, because we fill ourselves with more dopamine, we crave more and feel even worse when our body replies with more pain.

    Repetition can lead to tolerance and worse rebounds. In other words, we end up needing more of our devices to feel the same effect. And when we let go, we basically crash. We are afraid of that crash, so we keep pressing down on pleasure – and with our modern devices, that is incredibly easy!

    So how can we use this knowledge to solve the problem?

    The Willpower Trap#

    A bogus solution is:

    “Oh, I just need a little bit more discipline. Then I’ll shrug off that addictive stuff.”

    How often does that actually work?

    For me, I hardly lasted a day. Later on, I could not even last one sitting.

    But why? Trying to muscle your way through with willpower often fails for two reasons:

    1. Our habits overpower momentary intentions.

    2. Cravings mess with our decision-making. We can’t accurately judge things when we have a skewed perspective.

    This is especially true if you are trying to forcibly “lock in” over and over again. As Lembke says:

    “In the throes of desire, there is no deciding.”

    The DOPAMINE Framework#

    Here is what works better.

    The acronym DOPAMINE is the outline of our solution.

    This is inspired by Dopamine Nation and Digital Minimalism.

    D — Data#

    Ask yourself:

    What are you using, how much are you using it, and how often?

    You need to know what is actually happening before you can fix it.

    Do not guess. Track it, because we tend to be inaccurate with this.

    O — Objectives#

    Ask yourself:

    What are your objectives for using this thing?

    Even seemingly “irrational” behavior is usually rooted in some personal logic.

    Personally, this step was the most important one for me when I was trying to get out of my YouTube addiction.

    Here is what I asked myself:

    “What deep value is this piece of technology serving?”

    For me, I scrolled YouTube because I wanted to be smart, and of course I want to be smart! My ego would love it if I could change the world with my knowledge.

    But then we must take into account our problems…

    P — Problems#

    Then I asked:

    “Is this the best way? Do the benefits outweigh the costs?”

    Clearly, no! I was getting great knowledge from wonderful people in the first hour. But then I would go watch cat videos for the next five hours!

    The costs outweighed the benefits. But most of us cannot see the full extent of the consequences while we are using.

    Judging while we’re in the middle of it is a trap. You need to step back and look at the cost clearly, when our bodies have restored balance and not when our bruised ego is screaming at us. That’s the next step.

    A — Abstinence#

    Abstinence can reveal hidden problems, because we become impartial judges when our bodies haven’t restored our pain-pleasure balance. So we must do this “big” step:

    Try not using the thing for a month.

    “Wait bro, we don’t have the time for that!”

    Yes, but think about your future self. Do you still want to be bingeing ten years from now?”

    Probably not, so isn’t it great to do it for a month and end this?

    M — Mindfulness#

    Mindfulness means observing what your brain is doing while you are abstaining, without judgment.

    That means no: “Ewww! Why would I be thinking about thought x,y,z? I’m a loser. I’m a freak.”

    You are supposed to feel bad when you stop. Your body is just restoring its pleasure-pain balance. Then we get…

    I — Insight#

    Insights are the realizations you get when you abstain.

    These insights are often not possible while you continue scrolling. Again, you need distance before you can see clearly.

    N — Next Steps#

    Then ask yourself whether you want to continue using, stop completely, or use less.

    Most people either stop or reduce their usage, because the choice become obvious.

    E — Experiment#

    Take small steps with trial and error. Gradually figure out what works and what does not.

    Binding Yourself#

    You can use self-binding tactics to protect yourself before desire takes over.

    Self-binding can be:

    1. Physical — physically blocking access.

    2. Chronological — limiting use by time.

    3. Categorical — removing an entire category of temptation.

    Many suggestions can fit into multiple categories. Remember, trying to fight temptation head-on is usually a recipe for disaster and it doesn’t work well because willpower is not always on call.

    Bind yourself while you still possess the capacity for voluntary choice, because in the throes of desire, there is no deciding.

    Physical Locks#

    Physical locks prevent you from using something.

    They are simple and effective, though they may hurt a bit.

    Suggestions#

    You can lock your devices away, but I like to give it to someone else.

    I love Cal Newport’s practice of giving my phone to my friends. That way, I can still be available for emergencies, but I cannot compulsively check my phone.

    I have also heard great things about kSafe kitchen safes, although I have not personally tried them. A kSafe literally locks itself, and you can only access what is inside when the timer runs out.

    Warning: Make sure the barrier is actually secure! Do not just throw your phone across the room and then pick it back up five minutes later to scroll.

    Locking by Time#

    This means you can use the thing only when the clock runs down or when you cross a self-designated finish line. This helps because high-dopamine goods, like our phones, mess with our ability to delay gratification, and we tend to overvalue the short term and undervalue the long term.

    More suggestions#

    Get a good environment!

    Work by a timer with friends, because if they catch you slacking, then you get embarrassed.

    Categorical Binding#

    Categorical binding means preventing entire categories of dopamine sources, not just one specific part.

    This can also block possible triggers.

    Suggestion#

    Use the question from earlier:

    “Is this the best way? Do the benefits outweigh the costs?”

    For example, I may get great knowledge from wonderful people in the first hour of YouTube.

    But then I might go watch cat videos for the next five hours.

    So for me, I may need to ban all of YouTube, because I do not want to deal with those problems.

    Cautions#

    Do not substitute one addiction with another.

    Do not swing to harsh extremes. Take small steps.

    Seek balance, not self-punishment. Remember the homeostasis idea, so it’s ok to feel bad.

    Binding ourselves doesn’t work all the time.

    Final Reminder#

    If you want to stop bingeing YouTube, do not just fight cravings in the moment.

    Understand the pleasure-pain cycle.

    Accept some boredom.

    Build systems that protect you before desire takes over, because those algorithms are beautifully addictive.
