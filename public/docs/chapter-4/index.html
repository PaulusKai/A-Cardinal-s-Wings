    4.0 Procrastinating? Put First Things First#

    TLDR#

    Being able to put first things first is needed if you want to get good work done.

    We can make it easier by setting ourselves up to avoid our internal battles.

    Some good suggestions:

    1. clarify the task and why you care about it
    2. choose a good environment that’ll help not hinder
    3. focus on one thing at a time (try not to multitask)
    4. write out your excuses
    5. take small and consistent steps

    (These ideas are inspired by 7 Habits, Deep Work, and Digital Minimalism.)

    The Problem#

    When we have important tasks, we might proceed like this:

    1. “I’m going to get all my homework done!” 2)But then the distractions come in, like that new YouTube video, or our friends sit next to us and have fun, or we start multitasking with something that popped up.

    2. And when our delegated word expires, we stare at the ceiling in disappointment (“bro! I didn’t get anything done!”

    This happened to us a lot, so what do we think? How can we

    1. not procrastinate
    2. avoid distractions
    3. and focus on the important things and not something else?

    We are not advocating for an anti-fun policy or declaring that we must become a hermit if we want to get our stuff done. Who likes that?

    But when we need to get something done, we should avoid distracting ourselves as much as possible until we finish. Oh what about “just muster up that discipline!”? – isn’t consistent enough to be reliable. More on that later.

    So What Are Some Good Methods?#

    1. Focus on prioritizing tasks throughout the day instead of relying only on rigid time schedules. When we prioritize something, we do it as if our life depended on it (so that means we should ONLY do that, right?)

    We find it easier to plan by setting priorities each day/week, but we also have to admit that we cannot have everything at the same time. (Like why would you stuff your backpack full of books when we realistically have time for 1? I killed my back for the presence of more books, and I didn’t get any smarter lol.)

    1. Multiple times per day, you may need to weigh the benefits of focused work against the other stuff. Actually writing it down helps, because it’s easier to spot bogus reasons on paper than in thoughts.

    A Simple Example#

    “Write college applications at the library after school.”

    Then focus on one thing at a time.

    No multitasking, because it’s a horrible way to work.

    Or…

    Locking in for homework during class time, then talking with friends afterward, is better than half-doing homework while yapping with friends.

    To be honest, half-doing homework while talking with friends looks really nice.

    But I know my grade probably will not.

    Willpower Alone Is Not Enough#

    Relying on willpower alone does not work consistently.

    If you tell yourself:

    “Oh, I just need a little bit more discipline, then I will get it done.”

    Then be prepared to face the mirror at night and realize the whole day passed without anything getting done.

    Yeah, I feel you. That has happened an embarrassingly high number of times to me.

    “Well, why doesn’t it work?”

    Our daily willpower varies, and it is limited: some days we feel like going full sicko mode.

    On the other hand, when we are tired because we’ve used a lot of attention throughout the day, that’s called ego depletion. Our willpower goes poof!

    So is there a better way for using our willpower?

    Use willpower for things that actually matter. Block off unnecessary things so we don’t have to burn it fighting out distractions.

    (It’s like saving your heavy mana-cost spells/ults by using auto attacks on those weaker enemies!)

    Avoid the Battle Before It Starts#

    If we are constantly battling to focus, our easier habits will eventually win over the harder but more beneficial habits; that’s known as the law of least effort (Deep Work.) That’s the unfortunate reality.

    It is not worth using a bunch of willpower to fight off distractions that could have been avoided beforehand.

    So how can we avoid unnecessary distractions before we get tempted? How can we AVOID, not fight it? (Or, in gaming terms, how can we use our auto attacks?)

    1. Make Your Priorities Clear#

    Make it clear what your priorities are. Then clarify why something is important to you, because a big YES to what is important helps you say no to things that aren’t.

    So ask yourself:

    “What actually matters right now?”

    A good reason you can use is:

    “In my funeral, do I want myself to be remembered for this?”

    Let’s go deeper:

    Start with “Why am I doing this?”#

    When I’m tempted to procrastinate, I like to ask myself:

    “Why would I choose to put my most important task at the mercy of something less important?”

    And also:

    “If this really was important to me, why would I choose to hurt myself? By not doing it?”

    That question hurts a little, but it works. It reminds me that procrastination is not just “being lazy.”

    Sometimes it is choosing something less important over something I actually care about. And like the Muller-Lyer Illusion, it still feels right to get distracted, but hey we know the truth!

    Well, what else can we do to help ourselves?

    2. Go to a Better Environment#

    Now that you know that what you’re doing actually matters (or not), automate your focus by going to a good environment.

    Let the environment deal with the distractions, not your tired, worried, afterschool self! (From Models of the Mind, this method of letting smth external deal with the trouble is called externalization.)

    For example:

    Lock in during class or work in a library.

    When your environment supports the thing you want to do, you do not need to fight as hard.

    A good environment makes the right action easier; a bad environment makes the wrong action easier. Again from above, choose an environment that’ll deal with the distractions so that our weak selves don’t need to do it.

    3. Focus on One Thing at a Time#

    Do not try to do homework, text people, watch videos, and philosophize about the meaning of life at the same time.

    That is usually fake productivity, and it never worked for me.

    If you need to focus, remove the distractions before you start.Throw your phone out the window before you get to work. Ok, maybe do not actually throw it out the window. But you get the point.

    Do that thing ONLY. Talk later after you’re done. If you have thoughts that come up either ignore it or write it down quickly and get back; don’t ride on the internal roller coaster of thoughts when you haven’t finished what you need to do.

    A Useful Anti-Procrastination Exercise#

    This exercise has helped me a lot.

    Ask yourself:

    1. What have I been procrastinating on?

    2. Why?

    3. What is stopping me now or later?

    4. What is something I can do right now?

    Final Reminder#

    You do not beat procrastination by only yelling at yourself to have more discipline.

    You beat it by making the important thing clear, choosing a better environment, and focusing on one thing at a time.
