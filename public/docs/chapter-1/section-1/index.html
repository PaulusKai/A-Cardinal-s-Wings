    Changing Your POV#

    “Unless you make the subconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate.”
    — Carl Jung

    TLDR#

    Shifting your POV (I’ll denote it as PS for Pov Shift) is very difficult, but it is probably the most effective way to change yourself, and maybe others.

    Our paradigms shape our attitude and behaviors, which shape our actions.

    I like this example: if you see everybody in your class as an opponent, you simply won’t see opportunities that could benefit everybody — especially when that would actually put you in a better spot than your solo grinding!

    (Should have a later chapter on this, but tbh I don’t know if I have the time now.)

    PS: Very often, the problem isn’t out there, but rather how you’re seeing it.

    We often like to think that what we see is what is, but in reality, it’s what appears (Thinking Fast and Slow!) to us. We can choose to use the right lens — that is, the right principles — that will let us see what is.

    START#

    You’ve probably experienced an “Aha!” moment in your life. We are going to talk about those moments.

    More formally, we think an “Aha!” moment really is just a moment you obtained a new pov, or what 7 Habits calls a paradigm shift.

    What Does Shifting Your Paradigm Mean?#

    It means changing the way we interpret our surroundings.

    One way to see it is changing your frame of reference: from one frame of reference, an astroid’s orbit can be a perfect circle, while from another, the same one is a chaotic scramble. It just depends on whose pov you’re looking through — through the broader view, or through the pov of the little poor alien critter that’s getting dragged around the orbit?

    Again, you’ve probably experienced one already, like when doing a hard math problem or when reading a mystery book.

    A paradigm shift, or PS, is not a change in your attitude or your personality. Those are secondary pieces.

    We’re talking about a change in your character, deeper than any “technique.” That’s a primary, more fundamental piece.

    We see character as how you interpret and see things. Outside stimulus must first be interpreted by your paradigms/maps before you can use your attitude/personality to react to it.

    To interpret the world with a good POV is to adopt the POV of natural laws, or principles.

    I also see these principles as really powerful tools that have magic in them!

    According to Stephen Covey, these principles are universally used to build all nations and cultures; it’s just that they interpret them differently. For example, there is a universal sense of fairness, kindness, etc.

    It’s up to us to use them or not.

    Because of their natural status, they will always govern our lives regardless of whether we use them or not.

    Well, we suggest that you use them.

    Note from Nathan#

    POV shifts are good because they’ll help you get along with others a lot better.

    It makes you more empathetic, more pleasant to get along with, and also helps you regulate your emotions and not hurt people needlessly when somebody does something you’d consider wrong.

    Everything somebody does makes sense to themselves. Your job is to find out why.

    Example of Neglecting a Principle#

    Take a principle like:

    “You reap what you sow.”

    Let’s say all my teachers have taught me that I can only cram to win: I must only use shortcuts.

    Then when I have the opportunity to make friends, I’m only going to use “little tricks” to make them “instantly” like me, by faking interest and glazing them.

    Then over time, when they get to know me, they see through my fake persona and I’ll be rightly exposed as a fraud!

    “Only when your map is right, THEN attitude and behavior can work wonders.
    [So] we must look at the lens through which we see the world, as well as at the world we see.”
    — Stephen Covey

    Here’s an example showing why:

    Imagine you have just arrived in SF for the first time and need to find Lowell High School.

    The catch is you have a map of New York!

    Well, it’s easy to predict that no matter how hard you try, you won’t find Lowell High School.

    Ok, maybe after an egregious amount of time.

    If we don’t change our map, the attitude and behaviors we have don’t really matter.

    “Nah, I’m going to run faster!”

    You’re just going to run to the wrong place faster.

    “Nah, I’m going to replace my laziness with patience and positive thoughts!”

    Ok, let’s face it: by the 5th hour, you’re going to be sweating buckets and disappointed in yourself.

    Instead of hacking at the leaves of the problem — thinking that we are just lazy, etc. — we can cut the roots of this problem by getting a new map.

    The moral of this story is that you need the right map if you want to reach a destination, and that our paradigms are quite literally the maps we use to navigate our world.

    You see, the bad news is we naturally believe that the maps we hold to navigate life are showing us the territory.

    The good news is we can get a new map.

    When we have the right map, then diligence matters.

    Then being patient and being optimistic will pay off in the long run.

    If you have the wrong map, you won’t reach your destination.

    Paradigms Before Techniques#

    But we’re not suggesting that those secondary pieces like communication skills or being optimistic aren’t beneficial.

    Covey sums this paradigm well:

    “Perhaps, in utilizing our human capacity to build on the foundations of generations before us, we have inadvertently become so focused on our own building that we have forgotten the foundation that holds it up; or in reaping for so long where we have not sown, perhaps we have forgotten the need to sow.”

    Working on attitude and behavior without first considering your paradigm is like trying to get by your hard classes through cheating/cramming.

    You sometimes get by, even with good grades — Mr. Cooley class lmao — but if you don’t pay the price daily, you’ll never achieve true mastery and actually change your mind.

    When we change our paradigm to see that there aren’t any shortcuts, then it’ll become obvious to us that studying really is the best way forward.

    Studying smart is another thing — that’s in Chapter 5, if you want to join in that conversation!

    If you only see studying as slogging through material, then it’s hard to stay diligent over time.

    But when you see learning new material as fascinating, then it becomes a lot easier to be locked in, and thus get better results!

    A Big Paradigm Shift from a Dying Bishop#

    When I was young and free and my imagination had no limits, I dreamed of changing the world.

    As I grew older and wiser, I discovered the world would not change, so I shortened my sights somewhat and decided to change only my country.

    But it, too, seemed immovable.

    As I grew into my twilight years, in one last desperate attempt, I settled for changing only my family, those closest to me.

    But alas, they would have none of it.

    And now as I lie on my deathbed, I suddenly realize:

    If I had only changed myself first, then by example I would have changed my family.

    From their inspiration and encouragement, I would then have been able to better my country.

    And, who knows, I may have even changed the world.

    And the most effective way to change yourself is to change your paradigm.

    Grab a New Map#

    By changing our paradigms to reflect reality, we can do things that actually work!

    I, Justin, ask myself a lot:

    How accurate is my map?

    A word of warning though: due to our tendency to treat our maps as realities, changing them will hurt!

    This is the same when we try to change other people’s perceptions by convincing them.

    Even though we/you are right, it hurts for our realities to get destroyed, despite it being worth it in the long term.

    If something bothers you, see if you’re looking at the wrong place.

    Try changing the way you see the issue.

    We like to treat our maps as realities, but more often than we’d like, they aren’t. (If you want a good demonstration of our tendency to treat what we see as reality, look up “Muller-Lyer Illusion”. Even though the lines are the same length, we still see them as unequal! Isn’t that crazy?

    Know that all of us can be wrong, so look to grab a new map!

    A natural question now would be:

    “Now HOW do we get new maps?”

    That’s the next chapter.
