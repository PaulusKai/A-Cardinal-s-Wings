# 4.0 Procrastinating? Put First Things First

## TLDR

Being able to put first things first is a major requirement if you want to get good work done.

We can make it easier.

This chapter pulls from ideas in *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, *Deep Work*, and *Digital Minimalism*.


## The Problem

Some days, we have something important to us.

So we might tell ourselves:

> “I’m going to get all my homework done!”

But then the distractions come in during our focused time.

Maybe we want to watch that new YouTube video.

Maybe our friends sit next to us and start scrolling.

Maybe we start multitasking and pretend we are still working.

We are not advocating for an anti-fun policy.

Who likes that?

We are not saying you need to live like a hermit either.

But when we need to get something done, we should avoid distracting ourselves as much as possible until the work is finished.

The goal is simple:

**Do not procrastinate. Avoid distractions. Do the important thing first.**

---

## What Are Some Good Methods?

Focus on prioritizing tasks throughout the day instead of relying only on rigid time schedules.

Know that you cannot have everything at once.

Multiple times per day, you may need to weigh the benefits of focused work against the temptation of getting distracted.

We find it easier to plan by setting priorities each day.

But we also have to admit that we cannot have everything at the same time.

Plan out what important things need to be done.

For example:

> “Write college applications at the library after school.”

Then focus on one thing at a time.

No multitasking.

I feel like multitasking is a horrible way to work.

---

## A Simple Example

Locking in for homework during class time, then talking with friends afterward, is better than half-doing homework while yapping with friends.

To be honest, half-doing homework while talking with friends still looks really nice.

But I know my grade probably will not.

---

## Willpower Alone Is Not Enough

Relying on willpower alone does not work consistently.

If you tell yourself:

> “Oh, I just need a little bit more discipline, then I will get it done.”

Then be prepared to face the mirror at night and realize the whole day passed without anything getting done.

I feel you.

That has happened an embarrassingly high number of times to me.

Our daily willpower varies, and it is limited.

Some days we feel like going full sicko mode.

Other days, our willpower goes poof.

So is there a better way?

Yes.

Use your willpower for things that actually matter.

Do not burn it all fighting distractions.

---

## Avoid the Battle Before It Starts

If we are constantly battling to focus, our easier habits will eventually win over the harder but more beneficial habits.

It is not worth using a bunch of willpower to fight off distractions that could have been avoided beforehand.

So how can we avoid unnecessary distractions before we get tempted?

---

## 1. Make Your Priorities Clear

Make it clear what your priorities are.

Clarifying why something is important to you helps too.

A big yes to what is important helps you say no to things that are ultimately trivial.

Ask yourself:

> “What actually matters right now?”

Then act based on that answer.

---

## 2. Go to a Better Environment

Automate your focus by going to a good environment.

For example, lock in during class or work in a library.

When your environment supports the thing you want to do, you do not need to fight as hard.

A good environment makes the right action easier.

A bad environment makes the wrong action easier.

---

## 3. Focus on One Thing at a Time

Focus on only one thing at a time.

Do not try to do homework, text people, watch videos, and think deeply at the same time.

That is usually fake productivity.

If you need to focus, remove the distractions before you start.

Throw your phone out the window before you get to work.

Okay, maybe do not actually throw it out the window.

But you get the point.

---

## 4. Ask Yourself the Hard Question

An important practice I use when facing procrastination is asking myself:

> “Why would I choose to put my most important task at the mercy of something less important?”

And also:

> “If this really was important to me, why would I choose to hurt myself?”

That question hurts a little.

But it works.

It reminds me that procrastination is not just “being lazy.”

Sometimes it is choosing something less important over something I actually care about.

---

## Environment Steers the Odds

When you say yes to something that is truly important to you, and when you get into an environment where it is easier to do that important thing, it will not completely prevent procrastination.

You can still get distracted.

You can still mess up.

But it will steer the odds in your favor.

And sometimes that is enough.

## Real Reasons vs. Fake Excuses

Upon further inspection, I notice I have some fake excuses.

This is a good example.

### Fake Excuse 1

I am arranging a guest speaker for math club, but he has not gotten back to me.

This is a fake excuse because I do not need to worry about it until he responds.

There is nothing for me to do there right now.

### Fake Excuse 2

Another math tournament: Purple Comet.

This is also fake because I have done it since freshman year.

So why should I worry about it that much if I already know what will happen at least 70% of the time?

## A Useful Anti-Procrastination Exercise

This exercise has helped me a lot.

Ask yourself:

1. What have I been procrastinating on?

2. Why?

3. What is stopping me now or later?

4. What are the real reasons?

5. What are the fake reasons?

6. What is something I can do right now?

I am trying to constantly improve this for the future.

---

## Final Reminder

You do not beat procrastination by only yelling at yourself to have more discipline.

You beat it by making the important thing clear.

You beat it by choosing a better environment.

You beat it by focusing on one thing at a time.

You beat it by noticing your fake excuses.

And most importantly:

**You beat it by doing something now.**
