# 4.1 Bingeing? Scrolling Too Much? Solutions!

> “In the throes of desire, there’s no deciding. Binding ourselves is a way to be free from temptation.”
>
> — Anna Lembke

## TLDR

If you want to stop bingeing, do not just fight cravings in the moment.

Instead, understand the pleasure-pain cycle, accept some boredom, and build systems that protect you before desire takes over.

---

## The Problem

> “Oh I… just scrolled five hours of YouTube! AHH… I feel like a loser! Like bro, it’s not even fun after the third hour, and yet I still do this!”

We feel you.

We have done the same.

So how should we solve this problem?

A bogus solution is:

> “Oh, I’m just lazy and I’m just a loser.”

Nah.

You are not powerless.

You have the ability to change this.

Here are three keys that can help.

---

## 1. Understand Why We Binge or Doom-Scroll

Tech is designed to be addictive.

Platforms take advantage of our post-usage cravings.

There are papers and dedicated scientists locking in to make already addictive platforms like YouTube even more addictive.

They are trying to get you to watch “just one more video.”

They are trying to recommend content that is designed to be consumed, not necessarily content that is designed to help you.

Remember their goal:

**They want your attention.**

Your attention equals their salaries.

Their salaries equal their calories and dinner next winter.

When I play *League of Legends*, one game often turns into too many games.

Even after years of playing, pleasure never really feels like “enough.”

Isn’t that some toxic design, bro?

I hate you, Riot.

No, I love you, Riot.

You have given me an opportunity to help people.

---

## 2. Boredom and Loneliness Make the Internet More Appealing

Modern technology is built to be addictive.

But boredom and loneliness make it even more tempting.

Elisa put it well:

> “I truly believe the internet is so addicting because we are bored and lonely in real life. You need to have a very fun life for the internet to not look fun.”

Social proof also makes excessive doom-scrolling seem normal.

After all, we humans are social creatures.

More often than we would like to admit, we follow the crowd.

That is why it is important to stick with people who lift you up.

Tough clubs, teams, and communities can build resilience.

---

## 3. Feeling Bad After Bingeing Means You Are Human

If you feel horrible after wasting a few hours, or even a whole day, congrats.

You are human.

We often feel horrible after too much pleasure because of homeostasis.

Specifically, the pleasure-pain balance can help us understand what is happening.

Maybe you learned homeostasis in freshman biology class.

That was one of the only things I remembered, and it is one of the most important parts of this solution.

Thanks, Ms. Killpack.

---

## Homeostasis: Your Body Wants Balance

Homeostasis is your body trying to restore balance.

For example, when you get hot, your body tries to cool down by sweating.

When you get cold, your body shivers to warm back up.

When you exercise like a demon, your body replies by sweating buckets.

When you take a cold shower, your body may feel super warm afterward.

This is the important part:

**Just like your body balances hot and cold, it also balances pleasure and pain.**

If you press hard on the pleasure side by bingeing five hours of YouTube or gaming all night, your body may reply by making you feel miserable.

If you press on the pain side through something like working out, your body may reply by making you feel great afterward.

If you are in physics, you can think of it like comparing kinetic energy and gravitational potential energy when throwing a ball upward.

---

## Repeated Bingeing Makes the Cycle Worse

Our situation gets dangerous when we scroll and binge repeatedly.

As we fill ourselves with more dopamine, we crave more and feel even worse when our body replies with more pain.

Repetition can lead to tolerance and worse rebounds.

So we end up needing more of our drug of choice to feel the same effect.

So if we know scrolling is bad for us, how can we use this knowledge to solve the problem?

---

## The Willpower Trap

A bogus solution is:

> “Oh, I just need a little bit more discipline. Then I’ll shrug off that addictive stuff.”

That is a lie.

How often does that actually work?

For me, I hardly lasted a day.

Later on, I could not even last one sitting.

Trying to muscle your way through with willpower often fails for two reasons:

1. Our habits overpower momentary intentions.

2. Cravings mess with our decision-making.

This is especially true if you are trying to forcibly “lock in” over and over again.

In the throes of desire, there is no deciding.

---

## The DOPAMINE Framework

Here is what works better.

The acronym **DOPAMINE** is the outline of our solution.

This is inspired by *Dopamine Nation* and *Digital Minimalism*.

---

## D — Data

Ask yourself:

**What are you using, how much are you using it, and how often?**

You need to know what is actually happening before you can fix it.

Do not guess.

Track it.

---

## O — Objectives

Ask yourself:

**What are your objectives for using this thing?**

Even seemingly “irrational” behavior is usually rooted in some personal logic.

Personally, this step was the most important one for me when I was trying to get out of my YouTube addiction.

Here is what I asked myself:

> “What deep value is this piece of technology serving?”

For me, I scrolled YouTube because I wanted to be smart.

Being smart was something I valued deeply.

Then came the best question:

> “Is this the best way? Do the benefits outweigh the costs?”

Clearly, no.

I was getting great knowledge from wonderful people in the first hour.

But then I would go watch cat videos for the next five hours.

At that point, the costs outweighed the benefits.

This overlaps with the next step.

---

## P — Problems

Ask yourself:

**What problems are related to your usage?**

Most of us cannot see the full extent of the consequences while we are using.

That is part of the trap.

You need to step back and look at the cost clearly.

---

## A — Abstinence

Abstinence can reveal hidden problems.

Try not using the thing for a month.

It is intense, but it can help a lot.

It also helps to think about your future self.

Ask yourself:

> “Do I still want to be bingeing ten years from now?”

Probably not.

---

## M — Mindfulness

Mindfulness means observing what your brain is doing while it is doing it.

But do it **without judgment**.

That means no:

> “Ewww! Why would I be thinking about that? I’m a loser. I’m a freak.”

You are supposed to feel bad when you stop.

Your body is restoring its pleasure-pain balance.

That does not mean you are broken.

It means your body is adjusting.

---

## I — Insight

Insights are the realizations you get when you abstain.

These insights are often not possible while you continue using your drug of choice.

Sometimes you need distance before you can see clearly.

---

## N — Next Steps

Ask yourself whether you want to continue using, stop completely, or use less.

Most people either stop or reduce their usage.

The point is to make a conscious decision instead of staying on autopilot.

---

## E — Experiment

Use trial and error.

Gradually figure out what works and what does not.

That is where the next section comes in.

---

# Binding Yourself

You can use self-binding tactics to protect yourself before desire takes over.

Self-binding can be:

1. **Physical** — physically blocking access.

2. **Chronological** — limiting use by time.

3. **Categorical** — removing an entire category of temptation.

Many suggestions can fit into multiple categories.

Keeping yourself near temptation and trying to fight it head-on is usually a recipe for disaster.

We have tried it.

It does not work well because willpower is not always on call.

Bind yourself while you still possess the capacity for voluntary choice.

Because in the throes of desire, there is no deciding.

---

## Physical Locks

Physical locks prevent you from using something.

They are simple and effective, though they may hurt a bit.

### Suggestions

Lock your phone away.

Give it to someone else.

Personally, I love giving my phone to my study partner.

That way, I can still be available for emergencies, but I cannot compulsively check my phone.

I have also heard great things about kSafe kitchen safes, although I have not personally tried them.

A kSafe literally locks itself, and you can only access what is inside when the timer runs out.

### Warning

Sometimes the barrier itself becomes a challenge.

Then getting your drug of choice becomes part of the appeal.

So make sure the barrier is actually secure.

Do not just throw your phone across the room and then pick it back up five minutes later to scroll.

---

## Locking by Time

This means you can use the thing only when the clock runs down or when you cross a self-designated finish line.

This helps because high-dopamine goods, like our phones, mess with our ability to delay gratification.

We tend to overvalue the short term and undervalue the long term.

So do not rely on short-term decision-making when you are trying to break a bad habit like watching too much YouTube.

When we give ourselves time, we can evaluate our decisions better.

### Suggestions

Work by a timer with friends.

If they catch you lacking, then you get embarrassed.

Hopefully, that helps you focus when you need to lock in.

The kSafe idea can also work here.

---

## Categorical Binding

Categorical binding means preventing entire categories of dopamine sources, not just one specific part.

This can also block possible triggers.

### Suggestion

Use the question from earlier:

> “Is this the best way? Do the benefits outweigh the costs?”

For example, I may get great knowledge from wonderful people in the first hour of YouTube.

But then I might go watch cat videos for the next five hours.

So for me, I may need to ban all of YouTube, because I do not want to deal with those problems.

---

## Final Cautions

Do not replace one addiction with another.

I switched from YouTube to playing games.

That was not an effective strategy.

Do not swing to harsh extremes.

Take small steps.

Motivated by self-hatred, I once went all-in on being bored.

After one month, I swung back to bed-rotting and room-rotting because I could not stand it anymore.

Seek balance, not self-punishment.

Remember the homeostasis idea.

If you go too hard on yourself, you may revert.

Self-binding helps, but it is not magic.

It has limitations.

---

## Final Reminder

If you want to stop bingeing YouTube, do not just fight cravings in the moment.

Understand the pleasure-pain cycle.

Accept some boredom.

Build systems that protect you before desire takes over.
