# 3.0 Finding Your Way? Listen, Then Experiment!

## TLDR

> “Learn the rules like a pro, so you can break them like an artist.”

You are not wise enough to ignore everyone else. Don’t lean only on your own understanding.

Talking to others, listening carefully, reading books, and experimenting are some of the fastest ways to improve. Human beings are social creatures, and we adapt to our surroundings, so choose the best crowd and environment you can.

Spend most of your time learning from others, but still leave some time for solitude and original thinking. Personally, I like an 80/20 split: around 80% learning from others and 20% trying my own ideas.

Challenge yourself just enough. Like a rubber band, if you stretch too far, you snap; if you do not stretch enough, you do not grow.

When you are unsure how high to aim, err on the side of too hard.

---

## 1. Your Problems Are Probably Not That Rare

It took me a while to realize that many of my problems were not as unique as I thought.

For example, I tried to “deep think” my way out of my YouTube addiction from 9th to 11th grade. I spent years trying to solve the problem alone, thinking I was the only person who was “solving” a great problem of our age.

Then my brother told me he had basically been handed a better version of my solution by his college professor.

Feels bad, man.

That experience taught me something important: sometimes, someone else has already solved a cleaner version of your problem.

You do not always need to suffer for years just to rediscover what someone else could have explained to you in ten minutes.

I tried to distill my own solution, my brother’s solution, and ideas from books into a better system for dealing with scrolling.

The lesson is not “never think for yourself.”

The lesson is:

**Think for yourself, but you don’t need to rediscover fire when people are driving cars.**

Don’t trick yourself into trying to solo everything.

---

## 2. Reading Is Compressed Lifeforce

Why is reading so powerful?

Because authors have spent their lifeforce turning their best thoughts into something digestible for you.

Why do I love books?

A good book is years, sometimes decades, and sometimes lifetimes, of people’s experience, mistakes, thinking, research, and suffering compressed into a form you can absorb.

A bogus response is:

> “But I won’t understand unless I try for myself!”

Part of the art of writing is taking great ideas and making them accessible for you to understand.

So it is foolish to think you can easily outsmart wise people by refusing to read them.

Again, from the previous point, you don’t need to rediscover fire in the age of cars.

Trying to “solo think” the early stages of discovery often just becomes a horribly inefficient way to reach the same understanding as someone who simply read the book, listened to the teacher, or followed good instructions.

If you are rebellious, challenge the teacher **after**.

One of my favorite quotes summarizes this:

> “Learn the rules like a pro, so you can break them like an artist.”

---

## 3. Another Reason Not to Solo Before Listening

I made this mistake most obviously in learning math.

I was stubborn and afraid to ask questions. I convinced myself that if I could self-discover as much math as possible, then I would become *the* Lowell math genius.

People would praise me for my intellect, and I could goof off in class while getting straight A’s.

That did not work.

Instead, I kept banging my head against a wall that I refused to recognize.

I would tell myself:

> “Just one more bang. Just put in one more hour tonight and you’ll become an Olympian.”

But that was not grit.

That was inefficient pride.

There is a difference between productive struggle and refusing to use help.

A better approach is:

**Cover your bases first. Then try to discover.**

Read the textbook. Listen to the teacher. Ask questions. Learn the standard methods.

Then, once you have the tools, go struggle and use your grit.

---

## 4. Some Warnings

Change your worldview, but do not become someone who only consumes information and never acts.

As discussed earlier in Chapter 1.2, do not overlearn without acting. That is effectively amnesia.

It is usually not worth spending an entire day falling behind in a hard class just because you are trying to rediscover every single idea on your own.

Instead, put the material into your head from the book, teacher, or example, then use it.

Spend more time sweating than watching people sweat.

If you selected a good teacher, class, or book, the chance to truly lock in and discover something will come naturally.

And when it does, it feels amazing to solve a problem after a week of attempts.

---

## 5. Choose Your Environment Carefully

We are social creatures.

We adapt to our surroundings.

That means your environment matters a lot.

If you are constantly around people who are lazy, cynical, distracted, or unserious, you will probably absorb some of that.

If you are around people who read, train, ask questions, build things, and take life seriously, you will probably absorb some of that too.

So surround yourself with the best environment and crowd you can find.

Then spend lots of time learning what that crowd is doing.

Watch their habits. Listen to how they talk. Ask how they think. Notice what they prioritize.

But also spend some time alone every day.

Solitude matters because it gives you space to digest what you are learning and decide what you actually believe.

My personal split is roughly:

**80% learning from others, 20% thinking and experimenting on my own.**

---

## 6. Challenge Yourself Like a Rubber Band

Growth requires tension.

Think of a rubber band.

If you stretch it too little, nothing happens.

If you stretch it too far, it breaks.

Your challenge level should work the same way.

Too easy, and you stagnate.

Too hard, and you burn out or shut down.

Just hard enough, and you grow.

So how high should you aim?

When unsure, err on the side of too hard.

Not insanely hard.

Not self-destructive.

But slightly beyond what feels comfortable.

Remember that you have to move beyond your comfort zone.

---

## 7. Experiment Instead of Endlessly Pondering

As mentioned earlier in Chapter 1.2, action is one of the best ways to get new maps.

You do not always know what works by thinking.

Sometimes you have to test it.

Try a new study method.

Try waking up earlier.

Try asking questions in class.

Hell, even try sleeping in class.

Try talking to that random person on the bus who looks interesting.

Remember, you can’t really fail, because no one really cares.

The duration can vary.

Elisa might test something for a week.

I might test something for at least three days.

The exact number is not the main point.

The main point is:

**Actually test things.**

If you experiment, you are collecting information from reality, not just from your imagination.

Life is smarter than your ruminations.

---

## Final Reminder

You do not need to figure everything out alone.

Learn from others.

Read books.

Ask questions.

Choose your environment carefully.

Challenge yourself.

Experiment.

Then reflect.

Don’t have blind obedience, but don’t spam arrogant solo thinking either.

Remember, we don’t have all the answers.

Sometimes you just have to do things, get your hands dirty, and find out.
